Curriculum

Ten weeks, built to stay useful.

The course trades breadth for durability. You'll leave with habits of mind — systems thinking, data stewardship, responsible design — that outlast any particular model or vendor. Each week has one 90-minute live session led by three instructors (with guest speakers when their expertise bears on the topic) and 4–6 hours of independent work anchored in your own service.

Weeks 1–5 Foundation Week 6 Project 1 Weeks 7–8 Implementation literacy Weeks 9–10 Project 2 & capstone

Week by week

The 10-week outline.

Weeks 1–5 build the foundation. Week 6 is Project 1 presentations. Weeks 7–8 turn analysis into design. Weeks 9–10 bring it all together in Project 2 and the capstone.

  1. Week 1Foundation

    The AI shift: what is actually changing?

    From hype to honest reading. What has and has not changed about work, services, and decisions in a university because of AI.

    A concrete capability map of what AI is good, average, and bad at in 2026. The mindset trap of tool-first, prompt-first, demo-first thinking. The course's working definition of "service." Grounded in the NIST AI RMF and OECD AI Principles.

  2. Week 2Foundation

    AI in the university service ecosystem.

    Why "service" is the right unit of analysis.

    Service ownership patterns at a research university: central, distributed, federated, and "phantom" services no one officially owns. The four-layer model used throughout the course — people & roles, processes & workflows, data & systems, governance & policy. How AI changes each layer.

  3. Week 3Foundation

    Mapping services, stakeholders, workflows, and pain points.

    The discipline of getting a service onto one page an outsider could understand.

    Service blueprinting adapted for university contexts. Stakeholder mapping including the non-obvious ones — records, audit, accessibility, OGC, IRB. Workflow mapping: swim lanes, handoffs, exceptions. Surfacing pain points without blame.

  4. Week 4Foundation

    University data flows, stewardship, privacy, and governance.

    Every AI intervention is, at base, an intervention in a data flow.

    Data flow mapping: sources, stores, transformations, movements, recipients, destruction. Classification thinking using public taxonomies. Federal and state regulatory frames in plain language and at staff-relevant depth — FERPA, HIPAA, the Common Rule, ADA Title II, GLBA, state breach laws, export controls. Vendor and tool provenance.

  5. Week 5Foundation

    AI capabilities, limitations, failure modes, and evaluation.

    How to evaluate an AI output without simply trusting it.

    A current taxonomy of AI capabilities relevant to service work. The predictable failure modes: hallucination, plausible-but-wrong, confident errors, bias amplification, prompt injection, data leakage. Evaluation methods: rubric-based human review, gold-set testing, A/B, calibrated trust. The "outputs vs. outcomes" trap.

  6. Week 6Project 1

    Tier-1 IT Help Desk Triage — team presentations.

    A standardized scenario every team works on. Comparable feedback. Shared method.

    Your team maps the existing help desk service end-to-end, including the data it touches (the obvious — contact info, device info; the less obvious — screenshots with student names, error messages with PHI fragments). You identify three plausible AI opportunities, defend one (or none), and present a one-page recommendation to a fictional IT help desk lead. Twelve-minute live presentation with eight minutes of structured peer critique.

  7. Week 7Design

    AI-enabled service redesign and prototyping.

    From mapping to designing. What a responsible proposal looks like.

    Future-state workflow design: where AI sits, what humans still do, what changes for the user, what changes for staff. Prototype types matched to question types. "Responsible prototyping" rules: never use real sensitive data; document assumptions and limits; name the human in the loop; define success before you build. Translating a redesign into a one-page proposal a non-technical stakeholder can react to.

  8. Week 8Implementation

    Responsible implementation: risk, security, accessibility, change, adoption.

    The unsexy work that decides whether an AI initiative helps or harms.

    Risk assessment using NIST AI RMF Map / Measure / Manage. Vendor due diligence — the questions to actually ask. Security basics for AI-enabled services: auth, logging, secrets management, prompt injection defenses, data minimization, retention. Accessibility by default (WCAG 2.1 AA at minimum). Change management done honestly. Monitoring and decommissioning: who watches what, what triggers a pull.

  9. Week 9Project 2

    HR Onboarding vendor evaluation — and capstone studio.

    A harder discipline: evaluate a proposed AI-enabled change across data, governance, risk, accessibility, change management, and feasibility.

    A vendor has pitched an LLM-based "onboarding companion." Your team isn't building it. Your team is deciding whether, and on what conditions, it belongs in a large administrative unit — with a full implementation readiness package, a vendor questionnaire, and a recommendation memo. The second half of the session is the capstone studio: teams sharpen scope, plan the final two weeks of work, and get instructor and peer feedback.

  10. Week 10Capstone

    Capstone presentations and final reflection.

    A proposal a unit could actually act on.

    Each team presents a responsible AI-enabled improvement to a real (or realistic) U‑M service: service map, stakeholder map, current- and future-state workflows, data flow, risk assessment, accessibility considerations, human-oversight model, prototype or storyboard, implementation roadmap, evaluation plan, and stakeholder communication plan. Fifteen-minute presentation plus ten minutes of structured questions. Closing cohort-wide synthesis discussion.

Learning objectives

By the end, you'll be able to…

  1. Map a university service end-to-end, including stakeholders, workflows, systems, data flows, decision points, and pain points.
  2. Identify the institutional data involved in a service, classify it using a defensible taxonomy, and trace where it lives, moves, and rests.
  3. Evaluate AI opportunities across value, risk, and feasibility.
  4. Apply a responsible-AI evaluation framework (for example, the NIST AI RMF's Map–Measure–Manage–Govern functions) to a proposed AI use case in a university context.
  5. Distinguish appropriate from inappropriate AI use by data sensitivity, regulatory frame, and tool provenance.
  6. Produce a stakeholder-ready service redesign proposal — current- and future-state workflows, data-flow diagram, risk assessment, accessibility plan, human-oversight model, and implementation roadmap.
  7. Use AI tools responsibly to explore, prototype, summarize, draft, and analyze — and explain what the tool did, what it didn't, and how outputs were validated.
  8. Critically evaluate AI outputs rather than accept them — including spotting hallucinations, biased framings, plausible-but-wrong content, and outputs that mask judgment calls.
  9. Communicate AI opportunities and constraints clearly to both technical and non-technical stakeholders.
  10. Reason about implementation realities: vendor due diligence, procurement, integration, accessibility, change management, training, monitoring, decommissioning.
  11. Reflect on how AI changes your own role — what new judgment is required, what new skills are needed, what work shifts, and what your unit needs to do to support staff through change.
  12. Recognize when not to use AI — and articulate that decision in terms a stakeholder, auditor, or leader can engage with.
