Who it's for

A cohort deliberately mixed.

Real AI work at a research university requires service managers and developers, analysts and IT leads, data stewards and front-line operations staff to argue through the same questions together. This course puts them in the same cohort on purpose.

A tech role?

For the people who build the systems behind U‑M's work.

You already know how to ship software. The course is about the questions that sit above the code: what the service is actually for, whose data runs through it, what happens when a model gets it wrong, and how a proposal survives a security, privacy, accessibility, and procurement review intact.

Technical

Developers and technical staff

You build or maintain the systems behind university services, and you want to think rigorously about where AI fits in them — to be the person on the team who asks the "should we?" question before "can we?"

Technical

IT leads and infrastructure staff

Central or unit IT. You're being asked to evaluate AI tools, integrations, and architectural change in a landscape that shifts faster than the procurement cycle. You need durable frames for that work.

Technical

Data stewards and analysts

You own or curate institutional data. You are increasingly the person whose opinion matters when someone wants to put that data through a new model. The course gives you vocabulary and frameworks for that conversation.

Technical

Product owners and product managers

For internal tools, platforms, or services. You translate between users, engineering, and leadership — and you're the person who decides what a "responsible AI feature" even means in your product.

A service, operations, or analyst role?

For the people who keep U‑M's services running.

Leading responsibly on AI starts with seeing your service clearly, understanding the data moving through it, and making defensible calls about where AI belongs. The course is calibrated for exactly that work — no coding required, and plenty of technical colleagues in the cohort to argue the hard cases with.

Non-technical

Service managers and service owners

You're responsible for an end-to-end university service — advising operations, registration, financial aid intake, facilities response, procurement, library services, research administration, something similar. You need to reason about AI the way you reason about any other service change: carefully.

Non-technical

Business analysts

You translate between operational and technical teams. The course sharpens the habits you already have — clarity about process, clarity about data, clarity about risk — and gives you the AI-specific frames your colleagues need you to bring.

Non-technical

Front-line operations leads

Your team's work is the work most directly changed by AI-enabled service redesigns. The course is built to make sure your voice is in the room when those redesigns are decided — not after.

Non-technical

AI-curious professionals and builders

You are not a developer, but you've been using AI tools to prototype, automate, analyze, or draft. You're ready to grow beyond "clever uses" into a working system view of responsible AI in institutional work.

What to bring

The prerequisites are modest.

What you'll bring

  • A real U‑M service you work with closely. Anything counts — advising, registration, help desk, research admin, HR onboarding, library services, facilities, procurement. You'll use it as the grounding for almost every assignment.
  • Comfort reading professional writing — policy, reports, documentation, the kind of material that circulates in your work already.
  • Willingness to present work to peers and receive structured critique. The cohort format runs on honest feedback.
  • Roughly 6–8 hours per week, including the live session, for ten weeks.

Good to have, not required

  • Any prior coding experience — helpful but entirely optional. Half the cohort comes from non-technical roles.
  • Hands-on familiarity with an AI tool like ChatGPT, Copilot, or Maizey. Most U‑M staff already have this; the course builds from there.
  • Authority to implement the proposal you write. Many of the strongest participants are early-career staff bringing a recommendation back to a unit that then decides.
  • A manager already on board. If your manager needs a briefing on what you'll produce and how it benefits your unit, email us and we'll send one.

Think this describes you? Apply.

Cohort dates will be announced soon. Applications are reviewed on a rolling basis so the cohort stays deliberately mixed across roles and units.
