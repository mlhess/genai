AI and the University Service

The future of U‑M's services runs through its data. Ready to lead it?

A serious, hands-on program for the people who design, deliver, manage, support, and build the services that keep U‑M running — and who are ready to think clearly about where AI fits, where it doesn't, and what to do about it.

What this is

A working program for changing how you think about AI — from "how do I use this tool?" to "how do AI, people, university services, institutional data, governance, workflows, risk, and human judgment fit together?"

Across ten weeks you and roughly forty-nine colleagues from across U‑M will map real services, trace the data moving through them, evaluate AI opportunities honestly, and design proposals your unit could actually adopt. Three instructors guide the cohort, with guest speakers drawn from across U‑M and the broader field. You'll leave with a way of seeing your work that stays useful long after any particular AI tool is replaced by the next one.

Built around a cohort

A learning community of U‑M colleagues.

Most of the real learning in a program like this happens between you and the colleagues around you. The cohort is the course: a group of U‑M professionals working through the same material, the same projects, and the same hard questions at the same time.

You'll work alongside roughly forty-nine other U‑M professionals drawn deliberately from across the university: advising, registration, financial aid, HR, IT, research administration, libraries, facilities, procurement, data governance, accessibility, and the many units that keep it all moving. Some of you will write code. Others will never have. That mix is the curriculum as much as the readings are.

Three instructors lead the cohort, joined by guest speakers drawn from U‑M and the broader field — practitioners, policymakers, and builders whose work bears directly on the week's topic. Projects are team-based; critique is structured and comparable across groups. By the time the capstone presentations happen in Week 10, you'll have the beginning of a cross-campus network of people who think the way you do about this work — and who you can call when a hard question lands on your desk next year.

Why now

The next decade of AI at U‑M runs through the people who run the services.

AI is changing how universities work — fast, unevenly, and often in ways the people closest to the work are the last to be consulted about. The question isn't whether AI shows up in advising, admissions, financial aid, research administration, or the help desk. It already has. The question is who, inside U‑M, can think clearly about where it belongs, where it doesn't, and what it means for the students, patients, faculty, and staff on the other end.

  • Services are the unit of change. A clever prompt isn't a service. The question is how AI changes what people do, what data moves, and who is accountable when it goes wrong.
  • Data flows are the substrate. Every AI intervention is, at base, a change to a data flow. You can't reason about the tool without reasoning about the data — and the regulations around it.
  • Judgment is the durable skill. Models, vendors, and techniques will turn over quickly. The ability to read a service, spot the risk, and design a responsible intervention will not.

What you'll actually do

Three real projects. Not simulations.

The course is anchored in three substantial team projects. Two are shared scenarios so the cohort can critique each other's work against the same facts; the third is a capstone on a real U‑M service your team picks (or is assigned).

Tier-1 IT Help Desk Triage

Volume is rising. Tickets are miscategorized. Some users get rerouted three times before they reach the right team. Should an AI routing tool be part of the answer? Map the service, trace the data (including the sensitive bits users paste in without thinking), and make an honest recommendation.

HR Onboarding Vendor Evaluation

A vendor is pitching an "AI onboarding companion" — a chat tool that answers new-hire questions from HR and unit knowledge bases. Your team isn't building it. Your team is deciding whether, and on what conditions, it should be adopted. Risk, accessibility, change management, monitoring, the full package.

Capstone on a real U‑M service

Pick a service your team cares about. Map it. Understand the data. Design a responsible AI-enabled improvement. Produce a proposal a unit could plausibly act on — with governance, oversight, accessibility, and rollout all thought through. Present it in Week 10.

A cohort, deliberately mixed

Built for the people who run U‑M's work.

The course brings technical and non-technical staff into the same room. Real AI work at a university needs service managers and developers, analysts and IT leads, data stewards and front-line operations staff working the same questions together. Every cohort is drawn from all of those roles.

You'll fit right in if you're a…

  • Service manager or service owner running an end-to-end university service — advising, registration, financial aid intake, facilities response, procurement, research administration, library services.
  • Developer or technical staff member building or maintaining the systems behind university work, ready to think rigorously about where AI fits in them.
  • IT lead evaluating AI tools, integrations, or architectural changes.
  • Product owner or product manager for an internal tool or platform.
  • Business analyst translating between operational and technical teams.
  • Data steward or analyst who owns or curates institutional data and is increasingly asked to evaluate AI uses of it.
  • Support or front-line operations lead whose work is most directly shaped by AI-enabled redesigns.
  • AI-curious professional or builder, technical or not, ready to grow into a system view of this work.

You'll get the most from it if you…

  • Work with a real U‑M service closely enough to use it as the grounding for your assignments.
  • Are ready to present work to peers and to receive honest, structured critique.
  • Can commit ~6–8 hours a week, including the live session, for ten weeks.
  • Come with ambition tempered by judgment — curious about AI, realistic about what it takes to deploy it responsibly inside a public research university.

What you'll carry back to your unit

Work you can use on Monday.

Each deliverable in the course is something a unit at U‑M could plausibly act on. You leave with more than a certificate.

  • A service map of your own service. End-to-end — stakeholders, workflows, handoffs, pain points — on a single page a non-technical colleague can read.
  • A data-flow diagram. Of the same service, with classification reasoning and regulatory notes that let a conversation with an IT security or privacy office start on the right foot.
  • A responsible AI proposal. Current state, future state, risks, accessibility, human oversight, rollout — in one readable package.
  • A vocabulary for "should we?" Language and frameworks for arguing, with colleagues and leadership, about when AI is the right call and when it isn't.
  • Practiced judgment on vendor AI. The questions to ask a vendor before the pilot starts — not after.
  • A cohort of U‑M colleagues. ~49 peers across the institution who now think about this work the way you do. Keep the group; keep calling each other.

How the course runs

Ten weeks, sustained.

Each week centers on one 90-minute synchronous session led by three lead instructors and built around discussion, a worked example, and structured peer critique. Guest speakers from across U‑M and the broader field join sessions where their expertise bears on the topic. On top of the live session, expect 4–6 hours of independent work: readings, a weekly assignment anchored in your own service, and team project time. Total commitment is roughly 60–80 hours over the term.

Weeks 1–5 build the foundation: AI's current capabilities, the university-service lens, service mapping, data flows and governance, and honest evaluation of AI outputs. Week 6 is Project 1 presentations. Weeks 7–8 build implementation literacy. Week 9 is Project 2 and a capstone studio. Week 10 is capstone presentations and final reflection.

Completion award

A $1,500 award for participants who finish strong.

$1,500

Participants who complete the course with a final score of 87% or higher and meet participation expectations are eligible for a $1,500 professional development completion award.

Eligibility is not guaranteed and is subject to final University of Michigan program rules, funding availability, applicable HR, finance, and tax review, and administrative approval by the program sponsor and relevant offices. The award may be subject to applicable taxes and reporting requirements. The course may be offered with or without the completion award depending on funding availability in any given cohort. Final terms will be communicated in writing to admitted participants.

The course teaches, and is itself grounded in, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards, and the federal regulatory frames that govern university data — FERPA, HIPAA, ADA Title II, and the Common Rule.
